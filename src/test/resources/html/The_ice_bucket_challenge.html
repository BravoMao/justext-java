Remember the ALS ice bucket challenge? It just funded a huge breakthrough

Nicole Morley for Metro.co.ukWednesday 27 Jul 2016 8:50 am

For a good few weeks in 2014, just about everybody’s social media profiles were full of people dousing themselves in freezing water.

At the time, the ALS ice bucket challenge was dismissed as attention seeking or slacktivism by cynics – but it’s funded a major breakthrough.

Money raised in donations following the viral challenge – as high as £75 million in a month at one point – have aided in the discovery of a new gene linked to the disease.

Project MinE, a campaign funded mainly by ice bucket challenge donations to the ALS Association, have announced the discovery of the associated gene NEK1 could allow for new potential treatments.

According to a paper in Nature Genetics, researchers part of Project MinE’s global gene sequencing effort identified a new ALS gene, NEK1, which now ranks among the most common genes that contribute to the disease, providing scientists with another potential target for therapy development.

ALS

ALS is a progressive neurodegenerative disease. The condition affects nerve cells in the brain and the spinal cord.

People with ALS lose the ability to initiate and control muscle movement, which leads to total paralysis and death, often within two to five years of diagnosis.

While 10% of ALS is familial, meaning it’s genetic, the other 90 percent of ALS cases are considered sporadic, or without a family history.

This was the largest-ever study of familial (inherited) ALS to date, it involved contributions from more than 80 researchers in 11 countries. It was led by John Landers, Ph.D., of University of Massachusetts Medical School in Worcester, Mass. and Jan Veldink, Ph.D., of University Medical Center Utrecht, in The Netherlands.

‘The discovery of NEK1 highlights the value of ‘big data’ in ALS research,’ said Lucie Bruijn, Ph.D., M.B.A.

‘The sophisticated gene analysis that led to this finding was only possible because of the large number of ALS samples available.’

She added: ‘The ALS Ice Bucket Challenge enabled The ALS Association to invest in Project MinE’s work to create large biorepositories of ALS biosamples that are designed to allow exactly this kind of research and to produce exactly this kind of result.’
